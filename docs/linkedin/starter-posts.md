### 1 — Energy (Tue)
Solar is cheap everywhere now. That's not the question.

In Pakistan, a renewable project's bankability isn't decided by the panel price. It's decided by three things nobody puts on the cover slide:

→ Who is the offtaker, and can they actually pay?
→ Can the power physically be evacuated from the grid?
→ Is the revenue currency-matched to the debt?

Get those wrong and the cheapest megawatt in the world is unfinanceable. Get them right and the financing follows.

The most bankable clean energy in 2026 isn't a mega-plant. It's captive C&I solar for a creditworthy factory — and the efficiency retrofit that means you never have to generate the megawatt at all.

Where are you seeing the cleanest risk-adjusted returns?

### 2 — PPP / Infrastructure (Thu)
Most PPP projects don't fail in the design room. They die in the lenders' credit committee.

After 20+ years on infrastructure deals, I see the same five killers again and again:

1. Risk allocated to the party that can't price it
2. Revenue "certainty" asserted, not evidenced
3. The currency mismatch nobody wants to name
4. The SPV and security package treated as an afterthought
5. An offtaker whose credit is weaker than the model assumes

Every one of these is a risk-and-evidence failure — not an engineering one. And every one is fixable *before* you bid.

The sponsors who reach financial close all did one thing first: they thought like a lender.

### 3 — Agentic AI (Wed)
There's a gap between what an AI agent does in a demo and what you'd trust it to do on a live engagement.

Here's where I've found the line actually sits today.

Production-ready: first drafts, summarising 200-page data rooms, checklist-based document review, reconciliations, monitoring.

Keep a human in the loop, non-negotiable: anything that binds the client, high-stakes judgment under ambiguity, anything where being confidently wrong is expensive.

The reliable pattern isn't "AI replaces the expert." It's: AI does the first 70%, the expert does the decisive 30% — and reviews the rest.

Used that way, a small senior team can serve a much larger, more diversified client base without dropping quality.

### 4 — Financial strategy / dispute (Mon)
A dispute is rarely about the contract. It's about which party can credibly afford to wait.

The mistake I see most: treating a commercial dispute as a legal problem when it's a financial one. Before the lawyers, ask:

→ What does delay actually cost each side per month?
→ What's the relationship worth if preserved?
→ What's the realistic recovery, net of cost and time?

Run those numbers and the resolution strategy usually picks itself. Most disputes that "had to go to arbitration" didn't — they went there because nobody modelled the alternative.

Resolve the economics and you often resolve the dispute.

### 5 — Real estate / credit risk (Fri)
"DSO is creeping up" is never just a collections problem.

In every business I've seen, a rising days-sales-outstanding number is an early signal — of weak credit policy, of customers in stress, or of a sales team incentivised on bookings instead of cash.

Working capital is the cheapest financing you'll ever raise. A few days off DSO can free more cash than a new credit line — with no interest and no covenant.

If you only watch the P&L, you'll miss it. Watch the cash conversion cycle.

What's the most underrated working-capital lever you've used?

### 7 — Energy / industrial efficiency (Tue)
Negawatts beat megawatts.

In heavy industry — cement, textiles, petrochemicals — energy is 50–60% of cash cost. Yet the highest-return capital project on the table is almost never a new plant.

It's the boring stuff: waste-heat recovery, variable-speed drives, grinding optimisation, basic process control.

Lower returns? The opposite. Faster payback, less permitting, less financing complexity, and it compounds every single operating hour.

The cheapest clean megawatt-hour is the one you never have to generate.

### 8 — Agentic AI / deployment (Wed)
If you can't cheaply check an AI agent's output, you can't safely automate it.

That single rule has saved me from more bad deployments than any model benchmark.

The firms getting value from agents don't start with a moonshot. They:

→ Pick one painful, well-bounded, high-volume task
→ Design the verification *before* the automation
→ Keep an approval gate on anything client-facing
→ Measure against the human baseline — time, errors, rework

Boring? Yes. It's also why their agents are still running six months later while the flashy pilots got quietly switched off.

### 9 — PPP / FX risk (Thu)
The silent killer of emerging-market infrastructure isn't construction risk. It's currency.

Debt and equipment in hard currency. Revenue in local currency. One devaluation and years of projected return are gone — and it never appears in the "base case" deck.

A model that ignores FX isn't conservative. It's incomplete.

Bankable structures name the exposure on day one — indexation, partial hard-currency revenue, blended/DFI financing that shares the risk.

If your infrastructure model has no FX section, that's not the model being clean. That's the risk being hidden.
